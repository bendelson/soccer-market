The global soccer inflation

An analysis on the market for male soccer players in the last twenty years.
By Ben Mendelson

Quick question: Do you also hate it, when people talk too positively about how things were "back in the days" and how they don't understand, why things changed the way they did?

Sorry to say, but this story is going to add to that narrative.

Another question: Do you still remember Thierry Henry?

The French national was a striker for Arsenal London and pretty succesful.
Back in his prime, twenty years ago, Henry was worth 45 million euros.

That meant: He was the most valuable player in the English Premier League.
Each bag of money in this picture represents five million euros.

Now let me introduce you to a Norwegian with blond hair: Erling Haaland.
He is 25 years old and the current Premier League player with the highest market value.
Since 2022, he plays for Manchester City.

Bayern Munich once tried to hire him, but they joked about having to send him to a hairdresser first.

Oh, by the way, his market value is now estimated at 180 million euros or 36 of our 5-million-bags.

So that would mean, he is now worth four Henrys.

Before taxes and inflation, of course.

Haaland's team mates from Manchester City on average are worth 38.2 million euros today.
Twenty years ago, the average player of ManCity was worth 2.8 million euros.

So today, the players are worth fourteen times the amount from twenty years ago.

Arguably, players today probably aren't fourteen times as good as they were twenty years ago.

The increase rather has to do with the involvement of big investors and the growth of the soccer economy bubble overall.

And this increase in player's values is not a singular English phenomenon, as the following graph shows.
It's what happened in all the big european soccer leagues since 2004: in Italy, France, Spain and Germany.

The soccer inflation

Prices for soccer players increased. So far, so normal. A liter of milk also costs more today than it did in 1974.

But when you put the price increase of a soccer league in comparison to the overall inflation of that country, it shows how the soccer market has dettached from the rest of the economy.

The following graphs show this development for England and Germany and their major and second league. The average price level of 2004 is set to 100%.

And you can see comparable developments in the American Major Soccer League, the Saudi-Arabien SPL and in Italy and Spain.

Players are now worth three to almost seven times the amount compared to some years ago.

Inflation didn't even double in the same time.
